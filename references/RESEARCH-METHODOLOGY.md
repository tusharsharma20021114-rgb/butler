# Research Methodology — Butler Reference

## Structured Research Process

### Step 1: Define the Question
Before researching, frame the question precisely:
- **What** do you need to know?
- **Why** do you need to know it? (decision, report, learning)
- **How deep** should the research go? (surface overview vs. deep analysis)
- **Deadline**: When is the output needed?

### Step 2: Gather Information
1. Start with what's already available (files, documents, existing knowledge).
2. Search for authoritative sources.
3. Collect at least 3 sources for any factual claim.
4. Note source quality: primary > secondary > tertiary.

### Step 3: Evaluate Sources

| Criterion | Good Source | Bad Source |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Authority** | Expert, institution, peer-reviewed | Anonymous, no credentials |
| **Recency** | Published within relevant timeframe | Outdated, no date |
| **Objectivity** | Balanced, acknowledges limitations | Heavily biased, selling something |
| **Accuracy** | Cites its own sources, verifiable | No citations, unverifiable claims |
| **Coverage** | Comprehensive, addresses complexity | Oversimplified, cherry-picked |

### Step 4: Synthesize Findings
- Group findings by theme, not by source.
- Identify areas of consensus and disagreement.
- Distinguish between facts, interpretations, and opinions.
- Note gaps — what couldn't you find?

### Step 5: Report

Use this structure for research reports:

```markdown
# [Research Topic]

## Executive Summary
[2-3 sentences capturing the key finding and recommendation]

## Key Findings
- Finding 1 (Source)
- Finding 2 (Source)
- Finding 3 (Source)

## Detailed Analysis
### [Theme 1]
[Analysis with supporting evidence]

### [Theme 2]
[Analysis with supporting evidence]

## Limitations
- What this research doesn't cover
- Areas of uncertainty
- Potential biases in sources

## Sources
1. [Source 1] — [relevance note]
2. [Source 2] — [relevance note]
```

## Summarization Techniques

### For Documents
1. **Read the introduction and conclusion first** — they contain the thesis and key findings.
2. **Scan headings** — they reveal the document's structure and main topics.
3. **Identify topic sentences** — usually the first sentence of each paragraph.
4. **Extract data points** — numbers, percentages, dates, and specific claims.
5. **Note the "so what?"** — what action or decision does this information enable?

### For Multiple Sources
1. Create a comparison matrix: rows = sources, columns = key topics.
2. Fill in each cell with the source's position on that topic.
3. Identify where sources agree (high confidence) and disagree (needs more investigation).

## Common Research Pitfalls

- ❌ **Confirmation bias**: Only looking for evidence that supports your hypothesis.
- ❌ **Recency bias**: Assuming the newest source is the best source.
- ❌ **Authority bias**: Accepting claims just because the source is prestigious.
- ❌ **Single-source reliance**: Building conclusions on one source.
- ❌ **Scope creep**: Researching endlessly instead of delivering a "good enough" output.

## The "Good Enough" Rule

Research has diminishing returns. Stop when:
- You can answer the original question with confidence.
- Additional sources are repeating what you already know.
- You've hit 3+ sources that agree on the key points.
- The deadline requires you to shift from research to writing.
